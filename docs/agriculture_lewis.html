Structural Transformation, Water Constraints, and Pakistan’s Agricultural Challenge

Zahid Asghar

The Lewis Intuition (Simple Explanation)

The development economist W. Arthur Lewis proposed a powerful idea about how economies grow.

Early-stage economies usually have two sectors:

Traditional Sector Modern Sector
Agriculture Industry / Services
Low productivity High productivity
Surplus labour Higher wages

The key mechanism:

Workers move from agriculture to industry.

The Lewis Mechanism

Agriculture → Industry/Services

  • Agriculture has surplus labour
  • Industry offers higher productivity jobs

As labour moves:

  1. Agricultural labour falls
  2. Agricultural wages increase
  3. Farmers invest in technology
  4. Productivity rises

This process is called structural transformation.

What Happens in Successful Development

In countries that industrialized successfully:

Examples include:

  • Japan
  • South Korea
  • China

The pattern was:

Agriculture employment ↓
Industry employment ↑
Agricultural productivity ↑

Fewer workers produced more food.

The Lewis Turning Point

The Lewis Turning Point occurs when:

Surplus labour in agriculture disappears.

At this stage:

  • Labour becomes scarce
  • Agricultural wages rise quickly
  • Farmers invest in machinery and technology

China reached this stage around the mid-2000s.

Why Pakistan Has Not Reached the Lewis Turning Point

Pakistan’s structural transformation is incomplete.

Key issue:

Industry has not absorbed labour fast enough.

Problems include:

  • Slow industrial growth
  • Weak manufacturing expansion
  • Informal urban employment
  • Limited high-productivity jobs

Result: Agricultural Overcrowding

Because labour cannot leave agriculture:

Too many workers remain on farms.

Consequences:

  • small landholdings
  • low productivity
  • low rural incomes

Pakistan therefore faces structural agricultural stagnation.

Water: The Binding Constraint

Pakistan agriculture is extremely water dependent.

Region Agricultural Water Use
Pakistan 94%
South Asia average 91%
MENA 85%
Europe & Central Asia 36%

Almost all freshwater in Pakistan goes to agriculture.

The Indus Irrigation System

Pakistan relies heavily on the Indus Basin irrigation system, one of the largest irrigation networks in the world.

However several problems exist:

  • water scarcity
  • inefficient irrigation
  • groundwater depletion
  • climate variability

Water therefore becomes a binding constraint.

What is a Binding Constraint?

A binding constraint means:

The factor that most strongly limits growth.

Even if farmers improve:

  • seeds
  • fertilizer
  • technology

Without sufficient water:

yields cannot increase.

Crop-Specific Political Economy

Agricultural policy is shaped by political interests.

Different crops have different power groups.

Crop Political Drivers
Wheat Food security politics
Sugarcane Powerful sugar mills
Rice Export interests
Cotton Textile industry

Policies often reflect political incentives, not economic efficiency.

Example: Sugarcane

Sugarcane is:

  • highly water intensive
  • relatively low value per unit of water

Yet it remains widely grown because of:

  • strong sugar mill lobby
  • guaranteed procurement prices
  • political influence

Pakistan’s Agricultural Challenge

Pakistan faces three structural constraints:

  1. Incomplete structural transformation
  2. Severe water dependency
  3. Political economy distortions

Together they produce:

Low productivity agriculture.

Big Development Insight

Pakistan’s agriculture problem is not only a farming issue.

It is a structural development challenge:

Slow industrialization
+
Water scarcity
+
Political incentives

= Persistent agricultural productivity gap
