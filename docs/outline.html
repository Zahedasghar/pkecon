Economy of Pakistan

Course Syllabus | Spring 2026

Author

Zahid Asghar, School of Economics

Published

February 16, 2026

0.1 Course Description

This course provides a rigorous, policy-oriented overview of Pakistan’s economy. Beginning with national income accounting and macroeconomic fundamentals, it progresses through detailed sectoral analyses — agriculture, industry, and services — before examining persistent structural challenges including poverty, inflation, fiscal imbalance, and unemployment. The course concludes by situating Pakistan in the global context and critically evaluating reform pathways for sustainable, inclusive growth.

0.2 Course Objectives

The overarching objective is to equip students with a comprehensive, analytical understanding of Pakistan’s socioeconomic realities. Upon completing this course, students will be able to:

  1. Develop familiarity with the key features of Pakistan’s socioeconomic landscape and identify viable policy solutions.
  2. Understand the historical and structural processes that have shaped current economic outcomes.
  3. Critically evaluate government programs and international interventions addressing Pakistan’s economic challenges.

0.3 Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion, students should be able to:

  • Identify structural and institutional challenges confronting Pakistan’s economy
  • Understand the macroeconomic, sectoral, and governance dimensions of economic performance
  • Analyse policies and reform programs with evidence-based reasoning
  • Compare Pakistan’s economic trajectory with regional peers (India, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Malaysia, China)
  • Evaluate contemporary policy interventions for inclusive and sustainable growth

1 Course Contents

1.1 Module 1: Introduction to the Economy of Pakistan

1.1.1 Overview of Pakistan’s Economic Structure

  • GDP composition: Agriculture, Industry, and Services
  • Employment distribution across sectors and regions
  • Pakistan as the world’s 26th-largest economy by PPP (IMF, 2025)
  • Structural informality: documented (~$340 bn) vs. informal (~$457 bn) economy

1.1.2 Historical Context of Economic Development

  • Phases of growth: 1960s boom, 1970s nationalisation, 1980s liberalisation, 2000s services surge, 2010s stagnation
  • Comparative analysis: Pakistan vs. China, India, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Malaysia
  • Decadal growth trends and shifts in sectoral contributions
  • Why Pakistan fell behind: missed structural transformation

1.2 Module 2: Macroeconomic Challenges

1.2.1 Persistent Balance of Payments Issues

  • Import-driven booms and recurring current-account crises
  • IMF programs: 25 bailout arrangements since 1950; the 37-month Extended Fund Facility (2024–27)
  • Remittances as a structural crutch: 9.4% of GDP in 2024 (vs. India’s 3.5%)
  • Foreign reserves trajectory and exchange-rate volatility

1.2.2 Fiscal Challenges

  • Low revenue-to-GDP ratio and expenditure rigidity
  • Fiscal deficit dynamics and the energy-sector circular debt (PKR 2,842 bn stock)
  • NFC Award, devolution, and the absence of provincial fiscal accountability
  • National Fiscal Pact (September 2024): rebalancing federal–provincial relations

1.2.3 Inflation and Unemployment

  • Hyperinflation episode 2022–24; policy-rate cycle (SBP cut 1,100 bps since June 2025)
  • Economic growth vs. job creation: the jobless-growth paradox
  • Structural unemployment, youth bulge, and informal employment (72.1% of non-agricultural workers in 2024–25)

1.3 Module 3: Sectoral Analysis

1.3.1 3.1 Agriculture Sector

Importance

  • Contribution to GDP, employment (~37%), and export earnings
  • Underpins rural livelihoods and food security

Challenges

  • Low productivity and yield gaps (comparative analysis with China and Egypt)
  • Water scarcity and inefficient canal irrigation systems
  • Limited R&D investment and slow technology adoption
  • Climate vulnerability: the 2022 mega-floods as a structural shock

Policy Reforms and Future Prospects

  • Yield improvement through precision agriculture
  • Water pricing and irrigation infrastructure reform
  • ADB Climate and Disaster Resilience Enhancement Program (2024)

1.3.2 3.2 Industrial Sector

Overview of Pakistan’s Manufacturing Base

  • Key industries: textiles, surgical instruments, sports goods, cement, fertilisers
  • Export concentration risk: textiles > 60% of merchandise exports

Challenges

  • Lack of innovation, branding, and technology upgradation
  • Heavy protectionism and import-substitution bias
  • Energy crisis: high tariffs and circular debt choking competitiveness
  • High input costs under FY24–25 budget tax increases

Policy Recommendations

  • Special Economic Zones (SEZs): performance audit and reform
  • Industrial policy shift from protection to export facilitation

1.3.3 3.3 Services Sector

Growth and Composition

  • Banking, telecommunications, wholesale and retail trade
  • Emerging sub-sectors: IT/ITeS, e-commerce, fintech, tourism
  • IT exports as a rising source of foreign exchange

Challenges and Opportunities

  • High informality and a narrow tax base
  • Digital infrastructure gaps and the spatial broadband divide
  • Low penetration of formal financial services in rural areas

Policy Framework

  • URAAN Pakistan Digital Pillar: DPI development and e-government
  • Regulatory reform to crowd in private broadband investment
  • Promoting innovation in financial services (SBP reforms, 2023–24)

1.4 Module 4: Productivity and Competitiveness

1.4.1 Aggregate Productivity Trends

  • Total Factor Productivity (TFP) stagnation across all sectors
  • Resource misallocation and the productivity–employment mismatch
  • Pakistan’s productivity gap vs. regional peers

1.4.2 Export Competitiveness

  • Structural barriers: duty drawback inefficiencies, import duties on inputs
  • Lack of product diversification, branding, and value-addition
  • Comparative export growth: Pakistan vs. India, Bangladesh, Vietnam
  • Policy levers: tariff rationalisation, export financing, trade facilitation

1.5 Module 5: Institutional and Structural Barriers

1.5.1 Extractive vs. Inclusive Institutions

  • Acemoglu & Robinson framework applied to Pakistan
  • Property rights, contract enforcement, and market regulation deficits
  • Inequality, rent-seeking, and elite capture

1.5.2 Governance Issues and Policy Failures

  • Flawed economic discourse: revenue measures over structural reform
  • Protectionism and anti-export bias embedded in the incentive structure
  • Political instability and policy discontinuity
  • Military’s economic footprint and institutional insulation from crises

1.5.3 Public Sector Enterprises (PSEs)

  • Aggregate SOE losses and fiscal burden (MoF Annual SOE Report, FY2025)
  • Performance audit of key PSEs: PIA, WAPDA, Railways, Steel Mill
  • Privatisation versus structured restructuring: the policy debate

1.6 Module 6: Economic Policies and Reforms

1.6.1 Industrial and Trade Policy

  • SEZs under CPEC: outcomes, challenges, and reform needs
  • Export-led growth models: lessons from East Asia
  • Tariff rationalisation roadmap and export financing mechanisms

1.6.2 Fiscal Policy and Taxation

  • Tax-to-GDP ratio: structural constraints and reform agenda
  • Expanding the tax net: eliminating the non-filer regime (proposed bill, 2025)
  • Reforming PSEs: reducing the fiscal liability

1.6.3 Agricultural Policy and Rural Development

  • Yield improvement, R&D investment, and technology transfer
  • Water resource pricing and conservation incentives

1.6.4 Services Sector Reform

  • Enhancing digital public infrastructure (DPI)
  • Promoting fintech innovation and financial inclusion
  • IT/ITeS export promotion strategy

1.7 Module 7: Technological Development and Future Growth

1.7.1 The Role of Innovation and Technology

  • Digitalization and AI: opportunities for industry and services
  • Technological upgradation in agriculture (precision farming, drones)
  • Pakistan’s digital infrastructure deficit and reform agenda

1.7.2 URAAN Pakistan (December 2024)

Pakistan’s economic transformation plan rests on five pillars:

  1. Exports — strengthening foreign exchange reserves and global trade
  2. E-Pakistan — digital economy and government services
  3. Environment — climate resilience and clean energy
  4. Energy — affordable and reliable supply
  5. Equity — inclusive growth and poverty reduction

1.7.3 Human Capital and Institutional Capacity

  • Pakistan Human Capital Review: building capabilities throughout life (World Bank, 2025)
  • Education, health, and skills gaps as binding constraints
  • Entrepreneurship ecosystems and startup culture

1.8 Module 8: The Way Forward

1.8.1 Policy Recommendations for Sustainable Growth

  • Fiscal consolidation without sacrificing development spending
  • Building domestic capacity: moving beyond IMF-mandated reforms
  • Promoting inclusive economic institutions over extractive ones
  • Enhancing competitiveness in global markets through export diversification

1.8.2 Learning from International Experience

  • East Asia’s developmental state model: South Korea, Vietnam, Malaysia
  • Bangladesh’s garment-led export miracle and its limits
  • The role of meritocracy and technocratic governance

1.8.3 Addressing Structural Barriers

  • Breaking the stop-go economic cycle driven by balance-of-payments crises
  • Climate resilience as an economic priority (IMF RSF, 2025)
  • Long-run debt sustainability and fiscal reform

1.9 Module 9: Conclusion and Review

  • Synthesis of key structural issues across all modules
  • Summary of recommended policy reforms and their political-economy constraints
  • Student presentations: sector-specific or policy-focused analysis
  • Open discussion: What will Pakistan’s economy look like in 2035?
