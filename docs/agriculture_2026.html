Pakistan’s Agriculture Sector

Structure, Challenges & Policy Trade-offs | Economy of Pakistan

1. Why Agriculture Matters

The Big Picture

Agriculture is not just farming — it is the foundation of Pakistan’s food security, rural employment, and export earnings.

Three core roles:

  • Income → 22–24% of GDP (direct + indirect)
  • Employment → ~37% of the labor force
  • Exports → Rice, cotton, fruits: ~18% of total exports

Start here to anchor the sector’s relevance before diving into data. Students often underestimate agriculture because it is seen as “low-tech.” Reframe it as structurally central.

Agriculture GDP Share Over Time

What the trend tells us:

  • Declining GDP share — but this is normal in growing economies
  • Absolute value of output keeps rising
  • Problem: the sector is growing slower than industry and services

Structural transformation: as economies grow, agriculture’s share shrinks. This is not failure — unless productivity stagnates. Pakistan’s issue is exactly that.

Agriculture Employment Share

The productivity gap:

  • ~37% of workers → produce only ~22% of GDP
  • Implies agriculture is a low-productivity sector
  • Workers in industry produce far more per person
  • This gap drives rural poverty

Lewis dual-sector model intuition: labor is in surplus in agriculture. Moving labor to industry raises overall productivity — but only if industry can absorb it.

2. Structure of the Sector

What Does “Agriculture” Include?

Sub-sector Share of Agri GDP
Crops ~38%
Livestock ~61%
Forestry ~1%
Fisheries ~2%

Key insight: Livestock is the single largest component — yet policy focus remains on crops.

Students are often surprised that livestock > crops. This shapes the policy discussion: support prices for wheat and cotton dominate the agenda, while livestock — which affects more households — gets less attention.

Crop Composition

Five crops dominate:

  • Wheat → food security crop, heavily politicized
  • Cotton → textile backbone, in structural decline
  • Rice → export earner, climate-sensitive
  • Sugarcane → politically entrenched, water-intensive
  • Maize → rising fast, driven by poultry demand

The Livestock Reality

  • Livestock = ~60% of agricultural GDP and ~11% of total GDP
  • Pakistan: one of world’s largest buffalo and goat populations
  • Primary source of income for smallholder rural households
  • Milk production growing, but value chains remain weak

Under-discussed challenges:

  • Animal disease burden (FMD, LSD, PPR)
  • Feed cost inflation
  • No cold chain → post-farm losses

Livestock is the silent majority of the sector. It is more resilient to weather than crops but heavily affected by feed costs and disease outbreaks. The 2022 floods wiped out significant livestock capital in Sindh.

3. The Productivity Problem

Pakistan Yields vs. Regional Peers

Crop Pakistan India China Bangladesh
Wheat (MT/ha) 2.85 3.37 5.42 3.13
Rice (MT/ha) 2.52 3.88 7.03 4.74
Sugarcane (MT/ha) 61 80 77 40
Tomato (MT/ha) 9.4 24.6 59.4 13.7

Key takeaway: Pakistan consistently underperforms China and often India — countries with comparable or harder conditions.

This is not a resource endowment problem. It is largely an institutional and input-quality problem. China’s tomato yield is 6x Pakistan’s. The gap is technology, extension, and input quality.

Why Are Yields Low?

Supply-side constraints:

  • Poor seed quality — certified seed use remains low
  • Over/under-fertilization (no soil testing)
  • Water stress + inefficient flood irrigation
  • Pest/disease burden, weak extension

Structural constraints:

  • Fragmented landholdings (avg. 6.4 acres nationally)
  • KP avg. = 3.6 acres → impossible to mechanize
  • Absentee landlordism in Sindh/Punjab → low investment

Small farm size is not inherently bad — Taiwan and South Korea developed with small farms. The issue is lack of cooperative organization, credit access, and extension services.

The Lewis Intuition (Simply)

Agriculture          →    Industry/Services
(surplus labor,           (higher productivity,
 low wages)               higher wages)
  • As labor moves out of agriculture → agricultural wages rise → farmers invest more
  • This can happen naturally if industry grows
  • In Pakistan: industry has not absorbed labor fast enough
  • Result: agriculture remains overcrowded and low-productivity

Do not get too theoretical. The intuition is enough: too many workers chasing too little output. Transformation is stalled. This is structurally different from East Asian success stories.

Pakistan is one of the most water-stressed agricultural systems on Earth.

This is a structural vulnerability, not just a climate risk. The entire agricultural model is built on irrigation from the Indus system — which faces upstream pressure from India and internal mismanagement.

The Indus System under stress:

  • Indus Waters Treaty (1960) → Pakistan gets western rivers
  • But: glacial melt accelerating → short-term surplus, long-term crisis
  • Groundwater over-extraction: Punjab water table falling 1–3 m/year
  • Canal system efficiency: only ~40% — rest lost to seepage

Water-Intensive Crops: A Policy Paradox

Crop Water Requirement Notes
Sugarcane Very High Most water-intensive crop grown
Rice (Kharif) High Punjab bans delayed to protect farmers
Wheat Moderate Winter crop, less pressure
Cotton Moderate Declining area → less demand

Policy trap: Water-intensive crops are politically protected despite water scarcity.

Sugarcane is the clearest example: extremely water-intensive, processed in inefficient mills, yet farmers lobby hard to maintain support prices. This is a political economy problem, not just agronomic.

5. Crop-Specific Political Economy

Wheat: The Political Staple

Why it dominates:

  • 65% of caloric intake in Pakistan
  • Government sets procurement price annually
  • PASSCO buys wheat → stocks wheat → sells at subsidy
  • Storage capacity: only 15% of annual production

Policy tensions:

  • High procurement price → expensive bread → urban inflation
  • Low procurement price → farmer distress → rural protest
  • Procurement delays → farmers sell to middlemen at loss

Wheat is the single most politically sensitive agricultural commodity. Any government that lets wheat prices rise or fails to procure faces immediate backlash. This creates fiscal pressure and market distortion simultaneously.

Sugar: The Most Politicized Crop

The sugar economy:

  • ~1 million farmers grow cane
  • ~90 sugar mills — mostly owned by political families
  • Mill owners delay payments to farmers (crushing arrears)
  • Government sets cane support price, but mills resist

The cycle:

  1. High cane price set → mills resist → arrears pile up
  2. Sugar export allowed → domestic shortage → price spike
  3. Import permitted → oversupply → next year’s cane price politics

Sugar is a textbook example of rent-seeking. The mills capture policy, delay farmer payments, lobby for export quotas, and manipulate domestic supply. Students should know this is documented — not conspiracy.

Cotton: The Decline Problem

Historical role: - Cotton → textile exports → largest export category - “White Gold” drove Punjab’s growth in the 1960s–80s

Current reality: - Cotton area: declined from ~3 million ha (peak) to ~2.2 million ha - Yield gap: Pakistan cotton yield below India and competing countries - Competition from polyester + pest resistance (Pink Bollworm)

Policy failure: - Seed quality not upgraded in decades - Extension services collapsed - No R&D investment to replace old varieties

Cotton decline is an industrial crisis disguised as an agricultural one. The textile industry depends on cotton. As domestic supply fell, Pakistan increasingly imports cotton — a country that used to export it.

6. Provincial Differences Matter

Not One Agriculture — Four Realities

Province Key Characteristics
Punjab Breadbasket. Wheat, cotton, rice, maize. Avg farm: 5.6 acres. Irrigation-dependent.
Sindh Feudal structures. Rice, cotton, sugarcane. Large farms (8.8 acres avg). Flood-prone.
KP Small farms (3.6 acres). Tobacco, maize, fruits. Rainfed and terraced. Mountains limit scale.
Balochistan Largest farms (22.7 acres). Dates, apples, pomegranates. Water scarce. Karez systems.

This table is essential for students to understand that federal agricultural policy often fits Punjab and is a poor match for KP or Balochistan. Provincial autonomy under the 18th Amendment means agriculture is now a provincial subject — but capacity gaps remain.

Post-Harvest Losses: The Hidden Problem

Scale of the problem:

  • Fruits & vegetables: up to 40% post-harvest loss
  • Grains: up to 20% loss
  • Estimated value lost: US$ 4–5 billion annually

Why it happens:

  • No cold chain infrastructure outside major cities
  • Poor drying → aflatoxin contamination
  • PASSCO can store only 15% of wheat production
  • Road access to markets in KP and Balochistan weak

Post-harvest losses are more solvable than yield gaps — they require logistics and infrastructure investment rather than changing farmer behavior. But the political payoff is less visible than procurement price announcements.

7. Climate Vulnerability

Agriculture on the Front Line

Pakistan’s climate exposure:

  • 2022 floods: 9 million acres of crops damaged, $3.7 billion agricultural losses
  • Increasing frequency of heat waves → wheat losses at critical grain-fill stage
  • Glacier melt: Indus flow volatile — too much water then too little
  • Locust invasions (2019–20): $3 billion in losses

Most vulnerable groups: - Small farmers with no insurance - Landless laborers with no fallback - Livestock-dependent households in arid zones

Climate is not a future problem for Pakistan agriculture — it is a present crisis. The 2022 floods are the starkest example. Students should connect this to both adaptation (new varieties, drainage) and mitigation (reduced agriculture emissions).

Climate Adaptation Gaps

What’s needed:

  • Heat-tolerant, drought-resistant crop varieties
  • Efficient irrigation (drip, sprinkler vs. flood)
  • Crop insurance — especially parametric insurance
  • Early warning systems for weather and pest outbreaks

What exists:

  • Limited variety development (PARC under-resourced)
  • Parametric insurance pilots — promising but not scaled
  • Climate Smart Agriculture (CSA) programs — fragmented

Parametric insurance pays out based on a measurable trigger (rainfall below threshold, temperature above X) rather than requiring farm-level claims assessment. It is faster and cheaper to administer — pilots in Pakistan show promise.

8. Input Distortions

The Subsidy Trap

Pakistan’s input subsidies:

  • Fertilizer subsidies: fiscally costly, often captured by larger farmers
  • Electricity subsidy for tubewells (agricultural connections): promotes groundwater overuse
  • Support prices for wheat and sugarcane: create crop bias

Who benefits?

  • Larger farmers with access to formal channels
  • Middle-men who arbitrage procurement systems
  • Mill owners who benefit from cane price politics

Who is left out?

  • Smallholders (<12.5 acres) who can’t access formal credit
  • Tenant farmers without collateral
  • Women farmers without land titles

Subsidies in Pakistan are regressive — they tend to benefit those who need them least. The political economy of subsidy reform is difficult: beneficiaries are organized, losers are diffuse.

Credit: The 80% Problem

Formal credit access:

  • SBP agricultural lending target (FY2023): Rs. 1.8 trillion
  • Only ~67% disbursed
  • Commercial banks: generally do not lend below 12.5 acres
  • Result: 80–90% of farmers rely on informal credit
  • Informal interest rates: approaching 50% in distress periods

The collateral trap:

  • No land title → no formal loan
  • Many smallholders lack updated Fard (land records)
  • Land record digitization incomplete across provinces

This is one of the most binding constraints on farm productivity. A farmer who cannot finance a seed purchase at the start of the season is forced into input-credit arrangements with middlemen — who then capture the harvest at below-market prices.

Mechanization: Progress and Limits

What has improved:

  • Tractor density has increased significantly
  • Thresher adoption is high in Punjab

What hasn’t:

  • Precision agriculture (soil sensors, drones) — minimal
  • Small farm size limits mechanization economics
  • KP terraced fields: mechanization not viable at all

Fintech opportunity:

  • AgriTech startups (e.g., Farmdar, Ricult) connecting farmers to markets and credit
  • Satellite-based crop monitoring growing
  • Drone-based spraying pilots

Mechanization is not just about tractors. The next wave is precision agriculture — using data to optimize inputs. Pakistan has early movers here but scale-up faces connectivity and literacy constraints.

9. Policy Trade-offs

Trade-off 1: Price Support vs. Fiscal Cost

If support prices are HIGH If support prices are LOW
Farmer income protected Farmers distressed
Fiscal burden rises Government savings
Consumers pay more (urban inflation) Market prices may stabilize
Crop choice distorted toward supported crops Farmers diversify

Pakistan’s experience: Wheat support prices have created a near-monoculture in irrigated Punjab.

The fundamental tension: support prices help farmers in the short run but create long-run traps. Pakistan cannot easily remove wheat support prices because urban bread prices would spike — but keeping them distorts land use and water allocation.

Trade-off 2: Food Security vs. Market Efficiency

State-managed food system Market-led food system
Predictable supply Efficient allocation
Prone to corruption (PASSCO leakages) Vulnerable to price spikes
Insulates poor from price shocks Requires strong safety nets
Fiscal cost of procurement + storage Lower fiscal burden

Key question: Can Pakistan build social protection (cash transfers) strong enough to move away from commodity price controls?

BISP is Pakistan’s social protection anchor. A credible shift from wheat procurement to cash transfers for the poor is theoretically possible — but requires administrative capacity, targeting, and political will.

Trade-off 3: Subsidy Reform vs. Productivity Investment

Maintain subsidies Reform subsidies
Politically safe Politically costly
Supports current input use Redirects funds to R&D, extension
Rewards inefficiency Rewards productivity
Fiscal drain Fiscal space for investment

International lesson: India’s fertilizer subsidy bill exceeds its agricultural R&D budget by a large multiple. Pakistan risks the same trap.

Countries that subsidize inputs heavily tend to crowd out public investment in research and extension — which would have higher long-run returns. The political economy strongly favors visible subsidies over invisible research budgets.

Trade-off 4: Small Farmers vs. Commercial Agriculture

Smallholder-first policy Commercial farming promotion
Equity and poverty reduction Productivity and scale
Politically popular Politically sensitive
Hard to mechanize Can adopt technology faster
Needs strong cooperative framework Needs land market reform

Pakistan’s reality: 80% of farms are below 12.5 acres. Any transformation must work with — not against — smallholders.

Land consolidation (as in Eastern Europe post-transition) is not realistic in Pakistan’s political context. The practical path is cooperative organization, shared services, and cluster-based extension — models that work at small scale.

10. The Path Forward

What Needs to Change

Five priority shifts:

  1. From input subsidies → productivity investment (R&D, extension, seed systems)
  2. From flood irrigation → efficient water use (drip/sprinkler, demand pricing)
  3. From support prices → safety nets (BISP expansion, price stabilization funds)
  4. From fragmented policy → provincial agricultural strategies (18th Amendment implementation)
  5. From trader capture → farmer connectivity (agritech, cooperative marketing)

These are not radical reforms — each is supported by global evidence and has precedent in Pakistan’s own pilot programs. The binding constraint is political economy, not technical knowledge.

The Technology Opportunity

Precision agriculture tools:

  • Satellite imagery → crop health monitoring at scale
  • Mobile-based soil testing (MIT-developed low-cost kits in pilot)
  • Drone-based spraying → reduced pesticide use
  • Digital land records → unlock formal credit

AgriFintech:

  • Value chain financing — lenders finance inputs, recover at harvest
  • Parametric crop insurance — fast payout, no claim adjuster needed
  • Farmer apps → market price transparency

Technology does not solve political economy problems. But it can reduce information asymmetries — between farmers and markets, farmers and credit, governments and agricultural statistics.
