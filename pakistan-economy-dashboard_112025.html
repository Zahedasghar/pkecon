Pakistan's Economic Crisis: A Growth Model That Has Run Its Course

Author: Farhat Ali (Former President, OICCI) Published: November 29, 2025 Source: Business Recorder

⚠️ Critical Economic Alert

Pakistan's state economic managers have officially acknowledged that "the country's growth model is broken" after decades of boom-and-bust cycles.

Key Official Statements

"Stabilisation policies have become a way of life... the country's growth model is broken."
— SBP Governor Jameel Ahmad & Finance Minister Muhammad Aurangzeb
"Pakistan cannot afford another round of 5-6% growth financed by cheap credit, subsidised imports, or unsustainable fiscal policies."
— Finance Minister Muhammad Aurangzeb

The Old Broken Model

  • Consumption-driven economy
  • Heavy external borrowing
  • Import dependence
  • Periodic IMF stabilisation cycles
  • Brief growth spurts followed by crises
  • High interest rates and suppressed imports
  • Extractive taxation without productivity

Structural Problems Identified

  • Stabilisation has become permanent, not corrective
  • High energy costs with no efficiency fixes
  • Policy inconsistency deterring investment
  • Volatile exchange rate policies punishing exporters
  • Tax structure disincentivising formalisation
  • Elite capture of economic policy
  • Weak institutions and stagnant productivity
  • Chronic fiscal indiscipline

Required Reforms

  • Shift to export-led growth model
  • Root economy in savings and investment
  • Focus on productivity improvements
  • Industrial policy for manufacturing revival
  • Create "representative growth" with jobs
  • Reduce inequality and expand opportunities
  • Dismantle elite bargains controlling policy
  • Institutional reforms for competitiveness

India Comparison: The Poverty Reduction Success Story

India's Achievement (2011-2022)

Extreme poverty reduced from 16.2% to 2.3% — lifting 171 million people above the poverty line (living on less than USD 2.15/day). This demonstrates that GDP growth can translate into broad-based prosperity when properly structured.

The comparison highlights that headline GDP growth means little without job creation, reduced inequality, and expanded opportunities for the broader population.

The Path Forward: Key Imperatives

Economic Restructuring

Move from consumption-driven to savings-investment-productivity model with export orientation.

Industrial Revival

Address energy costs, policy consistency, logistics, and competitive tariffs to enable manufacturing growth.

Governance Reform

End elite capture, strengthen institutions, and create predictable environment for investment.

The Bottom Line

Pakistan's economic crisis is systemic, not episodic. Recognising the problem is only the first step. The political class must accept that prosperity requires the price of reform, and the business community must understand that sustainable profits demand competitiveness. Without real structural change, Pakistan will remain trapped in self-inflicted boom-and-bust cycles that its 250 million people cannot afford.
